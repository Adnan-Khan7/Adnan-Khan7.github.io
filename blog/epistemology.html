Before We Argue About God, Let's Agree on How We Know Anything

2026 Faith & Reason 10 min read

I am a student of both science and Islam. These are my honest reflections — not a fatwa, not a final word. Discussion and disagreement both welcome.

Most debates about God fail before they begin.

Not because the question is unanswerable, but because the two sides are not standing on the same ground. One person is arguing from scripture. Another from science. A third from personal experience. A fourth from philosophy. They are not disagreeing about God — they are disagreeing about what counts as valid knowledge in the first place.

The Farahi school of thought, before making any argument about religion, insists on establishing the foundation of reasoning itself. What do we actually know? How do we know it? What counts as certainty and what counts as conjecture?

This post is my attempt to summarize what I understood from studying that framework — as a student, not a scholar.

The Two Subjects of All Knowledge

The framework begins with a deceptively simple observation: all human knowledge is restricted to two subjects.

Matter — the external physical world. The cosmos, elements, our own biological bodies. Everything that physics, chemistry, and biology investigates is an investigation of matter.

The Self — the internal I that perceives, feels grief, experiences joy, makes choices, and possesses a personality. Everything that psychology, philosophy of mind, and ethics investigates is an investigation of the self.

That is it. Every field of human study, without exception, is a deeper investigation into one of these two domains.

This is not a trivial point. It means that any question which falls outside matter and self — such as the ultimate origin of the universe, or what happens after death — cannot be answered by science or philosophy alone. Not because the questions are invalid, but because the tools available to human beings are designed for a different terrain.

How We Know: The Seven Innate Principles

Raw sensory data is not yet knowledge. When you touch a table, your hand receives a signal. Something inside you then issues a verdict: there is an object here, separate from me, that exists independently of my perception of it.

You did not learn this through education. You were born with it. These pre-programmed verdicts — innate inspirations — form the foundation of all human certainty. There are seven of them.

1. The external world is real. Every living being operates under the compulsion that the world outside the mind actually exists. Even a philosopher who argues reality is an illusion will instinctively avoid walking into a wall. This compulsion is not a logical conclusion we reach — it is a given we cannot escape.

2. The self is distinct from the world. The mind inherently recognizes itself as the knower, separate from the known. Without this distinction there would be no external world to perceive — only an undifferentiated blur.

3. Every attribute belongs to an essence. We never perceive redness in isolation. We perceive a red object. The mind is pre-programmed to anchor every attribute to an underlying essence. This matters later when we discuss God — whose attributes we can know even if His essence remains beyond our reach.

4. We have will and agency. We possess an innate certainty that we can choose. We do not need a logical proof that we can pick up a glass or leave it. Without this certainty no human effort would be possible — why struggle if you believe you cannot affect anything?

5. Our actions affect the world. We act with the pre-existing certainty that the physical world will respond to our will. The repeated confirmation of this through experience deepens it into total conviction.

6. Different things have different essences. By observing that one set of attributes differs from another, the mind concludes the underlying essences are also distinct. This prevents us from confusing ourselves with the chair we sit on, or with the God we seek.

7. There is a difference between good and evil. Humans do not merely have preferences — we judge. We feel pride when we act virtuously and shame when we do not. This moral discrimination is as fundamental to the human being as the ability to distinguish colours. It is not a social construct. It is built in.

Two Sources of Knowledge

Once the mind has gathered facts through perception, it does not stop there. It asks: what is it? How does it work? Why does it exist?

This movement from the known to the unknown is reasoning. It is the engine of all science and all philosophy. It operates through two paths.

Logical necessity: Certain conclusions are forced on us by the innate axioms already described. If there is an effect, there must be a cause. If there is an action, there must be a doer. These are not hypotheses — they are compulsions of reason. Newton did not decide to believe that something caused the apple to fall. The mind demanded a cause. Gravity was the answer it found.

Intellectual possibility: When the cause is not immediately visible, the mind generates possibilities and then looks for evidence to prefer one over the others. This is the basis of scientific hypothesis. You observe that galaxies are moving apart. You reason backward to a beginning. You call it the Big Bang. You cannot see it directly — you infer it from what you can see.

Both paths are legitimate. The difference is in the certainty of the conclusion. Logical necessity produces certainty. Intellectual possibility produces a preponderant opinion — the most reasonable conclusion given the available evidence, held with appropriate humility.

Where This Leads

The argument for the existence of God — developed at length in the Islam and Atheism framework that I am following — is that God is reachable through the first path. Not as a religious claim requiring faith, but as a logical necessity following from the same innate axiom that tells us every effect has a cause and every action has a doer.

The universe exists. It shows every sign of having begun. The beginning of the universe is an effect. An effect requires a cause. The cause of the universe cannot itself be part of the universe — that would be circular. It must be something outside, prior to, and independent of the universe.

That is not yet the God of any particular religion. It is simply the logical conclusion that something uncaused must exist. The question of what that uncaused thing is — and whether it has communicated with humanity — is where revelation enters the conversation.

A Note on Hume and Causality

The most common philosophical objection to the causality argument is David Hume's: we do not actually observe causality, only constant conjunction. We see B follow A repeatedly and assume A caused B, but this is a mental habit, not a proven necessity.

The response from this framework is direct: causality is not something we learn from experience. It is an innate axiom we are born with. Experience confirms what is already embedded. To deny causality entirely is not skepticism — it is sophistry. It destroys the very possibility of knowledge, including the knowledge that Hume's own argument is valid.

A philosophy that defeats itself is not a philosophy. It is a performance.

Why This Matters to Me as a Student of Computer Science

I work in AI. I spend my days building systems that take inputs, process them through learned representations, and produce outputs. These systems can now catch errors I miss, generate images, write code, and hold conversations.

They operate entirely within what this framework calls the domain of matter. They process physical signals. They optimize mathematical functions. They have no self that is distinct from their computation, no innate moral compass, no compulsion to believe in an external world, no sense of agency.

The distance between the most capable AI system ever built and a human child asking why am I here is not a matter of more data or better architecture. It is categorical. Something is present in the human being that is simply not present in the machine — and no amount of scaling closes that gap.

I do not think that something arrived by accident.

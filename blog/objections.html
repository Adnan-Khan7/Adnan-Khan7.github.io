If God is Real, Why Does a Child Starve? — Ten Objections and Honest Answers

2026 Faith & Reason 11 min read

I am a student of both science and Islam. These are my honest reflections — not a fatwa, not a final word. Discussion and disagreement both welcome.

This is the question that stops most conversations about God before they get anywhere useful.

If God is all-powerful and all-merciful — if He is both able to prevent suffering and loving enough to want to — then why does a child starve? Why does a tyrant rule? Why does an earthquake kill thousands of people who never chose anything?

These are not cheap objections. They are honest ones. I have asked versions of them myself. And I think they deserve honest answers — not dismissal, not deflection, and not the kind of response that requires you to already believe before it makes sense.

What follows is my attempt to engage with the most serious objections to God's existence and justice, drawing on the framework I have been studying. I am a student. These are my notes, not a final word.

Objection 1: The Problem of Evil — If God is Good, Why is There Suffering?

This is the oldest and most serious objection. Its core is always the same: a good God and a world full of innocent suffering seem logically incompatible.

The response begins with a question about what this world is for.

If this world is a place of final reward and punishment — if it is meant to be just and complete in itself — then yes, the suffering of the innocent is an indictment of whoever is in charge.

But the Quran does not present this world as a place of final reward. It presents it as a test. A temporary passage between two permanent states.

Do people think they will be left alone because they say we believe, and will not be tested?

(Quran 29:2)

Once you accept that premise, the problem of evil transforms. The question is no longer why does God allow suffering but why does a test require the possibility of suffering.

A test with no real stakes is not a test. A choice with no real consequences is not a choice. If God intervened every time a hand was raised to commit a crime, human free will would be a performance — a theatre of choices that never actually matter. The moral weight of human action requires that the action be real, the consequences be real, and the choice be genuinely free.

This is the distinction between what God approves and what God permits. God permits evil not because He is indifferent to it but because the alternative — a world in which no evil is ever possible — would be a world in which no genuine goodness is possible either.

Objection 2: Free Will and Divine Foreknowledge — Is Our Freedom Real?

If God already knows every choice I will make, am I really choosing? Or is the appearance of free will just that — an appearance?

This objection is philosophically serious and I will not pretend it has a simple resolution. But it contains a confusion about what knowing means.

Knowing the outcome of a choice is not the same as causing it. If I know my friend well enough to predict with certainty that she will choose coffee over tea, I have not removed her freedom to choose. My knowledge is downstream of her nature and her will — not upstream of it.

The Quran consistently, repeatedly, insistently presents human beings as moral agents who will be held accountable for their choices. That accountability only makes sense if the choices are real. I am not willing to dismiss the entire architecture of the argument because I cannot personally resolve the metaphysics of omniscience and free will within the limits of human cognition.

Objection 3: Natural Disasters — Why Do Earthquakes Kill Innocent People?

This is different from the problem of human evil because no human chose to send the earthquake. The objection is not about free will. It is about the physical laws of the universe causing suffering to people who did nothing wrong.

The response here is twofold.

First: the physical laws of the universe are consistent. They do not make exceptions. Water does not stop being wet for good people. Gravity does not suspend itself for the innocent. This consistency is not cruelty — it is the foundation of the physical world being navigable, learnable, and subject to human understanding. A universe in which physical laws made moral exceptions would be a universe in which science was impossible.

Second: natural calamities serve a function that is difficult to see from inside the comfort of ordinary life. They are reminders — sharp, unchosen, impossible to ignore — of human limitation and the temporary nature of this existence. They are part of the scheme that prevents human beings from forgetting they are guests here, not owners.

Objection 4: Divine Justice — Isn't Hell Just Revenge?

If God is merciful, why does He punish anyone eternally? Is eternal punishment not disproportionate to a finite life of sin?

The response begins with a question about what justice requires.

If there is no ultimate accountability — if the life of a murderer and the life of a saint are genuinely equal at the end — then justice is a fiction. Not just religious justice. All justice. The human intuition that some things matter, that some actions are genuinely wrong and deserve consequence, becomes a cognitive illusion.

And We will set up the scales of justice for the Day of Resurrection, so that not a soul will be wronged in the least.

(Quran 21:47)

Divine punishment in the Quran is presented as the legal consequence of violating a moral order that was communicated clearly, through Prophets, over the entire span of human history. It is not revenge. It is completion.

Objection 5: Abraham and His Son — Divine Tyranny?

A father commanded to kill his own son. What kind of God asks this?

The Quranic reading of this story is careful and worth presenting fully.

First: the command came through a prophetic dream — a specific mode of direct divine communication reserved for Prophets, categorically different from ordinary dreams or personal interpretation.

Second: the son in the story was not a helpless child. He was a consenting adult who understood the spiritual magnitude of what was being asked. The text records his response explicitly. He agreed.

Third — and this is the point that matters most — the act was stopped. God did not want the death of the son. He wanted the demonstration of total submission. Once that willingness was demonstrated completely, the command was withdrawn. The story is not about God wanting blood. It is about what total trust looks like when it costs everything.

Objection 6: Why is God Silent?

If God exists and wants us to believe in Him, why doesn't He simply make Himself visible?

Imagine a world in which God's existence was as empirically obvious as gravity — where disbelief was simply not an option. In such a world, obedience to God would carry no moral weight. You cannot be praised for believing what you have no choice but to believe. You cannot be credited with choosing the good when the alternative has been made impossible.

The test requires the curtain. The hiddenness is not abandonment. It is the condition that makes the choice meaningful.

Objection 7: What About People Who Never Heard the Message?

The Quran addresses this directly. Divine accountability is proportional to divine communication. A people who never received a Prophet, never had access to the message, are not judged by the same standard as those to whom the message was delivered clearly.

Upon such shall be no fear, nor shall they grieve.

(Quran 10:62)

Beyond that, the Quran points to the innate human nature that contains a moral compass, a recognition of good and evil, and a disposition toward the divine. Every human being carries this. It is the minimum ground of accountability.

Objection 8: Why Are There So Many Religions?

If God wanted humanity to follow one path, why is the world full of contradictory religious traditions, each claiming to be the truth?

The diversity is not evidence against God — it is evidence of human nature. The Quran presents one continuous message delivered through a long chain of Prophets. The diversity arose because human beings, over time, corrupted and fragmented the original. The Quran presents itself as the final, preserved, uncorrupted version of that single message.

The question then shifts: not why are there many religions but which tradition can demonstrate the integrity of its transmission. That is a historical and textual question, not merely a philosophical one.

Objection 9: Religion Has Caused More Harm Than Good

The Crusades. The Inquisition. Terrorism committed in God's name. If religion produces this much suffering, is it not a net negative for humanity?

This objection conflates the message with what people have done in its name. By the same logic, science — which produced nuclear weapons and instruments of surveillance — should be abandoned. The problem is not the framework. The problem is human beings who use frameworks to serve their will to power.

The violence committed in Islam's name is not an expression of the Quran's teachings. It is the consequence of a specific distorted reading — one that turned a spiritual message into a political ideology of coercion. The answer to that distortion is not to abandon the religion. It is to correct the reading. I discuss this at length in Why Muslims Are Not Flourishing.

Objection 10: I Live a Good Life Without God — Why Do I Need Religion?

This is perhaps the most personally honest objection. Not hostile — just genuinely asking: if I am kind, ethical, and contributing to the world, what does religion add?

The innate moral compass is present in every human being regardless of belief. Good people exist everywhere. The question religion answers is not how do I be good but what is the purpose of the good I do. Ethics without metaphysics can tell you what to do. It cannot tell you why it ultimately matters — why the difference between cruelty and kindness is more than a social preference, why justice is more than a useful fiction.

Religion does not add goodness to good people. It gives the goodness a foundation that does not depend on consensus, convenience, or the shifting standards of any particular culture.

The Thread Running Through All of These

Every one of these objections, when examined carefully, rests on an assumption that deserves to be examined: that this world should be a place of complete and visible justice, that suffering should be proportional to sin, that God should make His existence undeniable, that the test should be easy.

The Quran does not accept that assumption. It presents a different framework entirely — one in which this world is temporary, the real accounting comes later, the test is real precisely because it is difficult, and the mercy of God is expressed not in removing all hardship but in providing every human being with the tools to navigate it.

When I look at the consistency of that framework — across every objection I have encountered — I find it more coherent, more honest about the actual human condition, and more morally serious than any alternative I have been offered.

I am still a student. I hold these conclusions with humility. But I hold them.

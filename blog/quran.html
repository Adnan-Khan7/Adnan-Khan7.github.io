Reading the Quran for the First Time — At 28, in Ottawa, in English

2026 Science & Islam 10 min read

I am a student of both science and Islam. These are my honest reflections — not a fatwa, not a final word. Discussion and disagreement both welcome.

I grew up reading the Quran.

I read it in Arabic. I memorised portions of it. I recited it in prayer every day. I completed it multiple times in the way millions of Muslim children do — moving through the text with correct pronunciation, correct rhythm, correct respect.

I did not understand a single word of what I was saying.

This is not unusual. It is, in fact, the norm for a vast number of Muslims whose first language is not Arabic. The Quran is recited in Arabic as a matter of religious practice — and for many of us, the recitation and the understanding were never connected. We inherited the sound without the meaning. The form without the content. The habit without the comprehension.

I am not saying this as criticism of how I was raised. My mother gave me something real when she gave me that habit. The discipline of prayer, the rhythm of the text in my mouth, the daily practice of returning to something beyond myself — these were genuine gifts. I do not diminish them.

But I was twenty-eight years old, sitting in Ottawa, when I read the Quran for the first time in a language I actually understood. And it was not the book I thought I knew.

What I Expected

I expected rules. Prohibitions. A list of what is permitted and what is forbidden, with the reasoning either absent or opaque. I expected the book that its critics describe — harsh in places, confusing in others, requiring specialists to interpret and authorities to enforce.

This expectation was not entirely my fault. It is what the traditional narrative, described in Why Muslims Are Not Flourishing, had prepared me to find. A book of law. A manual for a theocratic state. A text that needed centuries of jurisprudence layered on top of it before it became usable.

What I found was something entirely different.

The Nazm — The Hidden Architecture

The Farahi school of thought begins with one claim that changes everything: the Quran is not a random collection of verses. It is a meticulously structured literary masterpiece in which every chapter has a central theme, every verse serves a specific logical purpose, and meaning is derived primarily from the internal coherence of the text itself.

This principle is called Nazm — coherence, or structural unity.

Before this school's emergence, the dominant approach to Quranic interpretation was verse-by-verse — each verse extracted, commented upon, and applied independently. This approach produced a vast and rich tradition of scholarship. It also produced fragmentation. Verses that seemed harsh when isolated looked entirely different when read in the context of the argument being made across an entire chapter.

Imam Hamiduddin Farahi spent his life establishing that the Quran had been revealed as a unified whole. That the beginning of each chapter, the middle, and the end were not accidental arrangements but a deliberate structure. That understanding any verse required understanding the chapter it belonged to, and understanding the chapter required grasping its central pillar.

When I encountered this methodology, something shifted. I was not reading disconnected fragments anymore. I was reading an argument. A sustained, coherent, internally consistent argument — addressed to human beings, in human language, about the most important questions human beings can ask.

Making the Implicit Explicit

Classical Arabic — especially the Arabic of the Quran — operates with a compression that has no equivalent in most modern languages. Connectors are omitted. Transitions are assumed. Logical bridges that were obvious to the original audience are invisible to a reader fourteen centuries later in a different language and culture.

The Farahi school's translation and commentary approach uses careful phrasing and contextual notes to make these logical bridges visible — not to add to the text but to make explicit what was always implicit. The result is a reading experience unlike anything I had encountered before. The flow of argument becomes followable. The why behind each verse becomes accessible.

I am not a scholar of Arabic. I cannot verify every interpretive choice independently. What I can say is that reading the Quran this way — as a coherent argument rather than a collection of pronouncements — changed my relationship to the text completely.

The Quran as a Chronicle of Warning

One of the most significant reframings I encountered is the definition of the Quran's primary purpose.

The Quran is fundamentally a Chronicle of Warning — a record of a specific historical event: the divine trial of the direct addressees of the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him. This was not a general address to all of humanity across all of time, delivered in a single undifferentiated voice. It was a specific communication, in a specific historical moment, to specific people — with the full knowledge that it would also speak to everyone who came after.

This reframing clarifies many of the verses that trouble modern readers most. Verses about warfare, about the treatment of specific groups, about punishments — many of these were specific legal verdicts delivered by God during the Prophet's mission, addressed to the particular situation of that community at that time. They are not general laws for all Muslims in all times and places, to be extracted from their context and applied universally.

This is not a convenient escape from difficult verses. It is a methodology — the methodology of reading any text in its historical and linguistic context before applying it to a different context. It is the same methodology we apply to every other text we take seriously.

Al-Hikmah and Al-Kitab — Two Tiers

One of the structural distinctions I have found most clarifying is between Al-Hikmah — the philosophy or wisdom of religion — and Al-Kitab — the law.

Most encounters with Islam, from both inside and outside the tradition, focus almost exclusively on the law. What is halal, what is haram. What the prescribed punishments are. This is not unimportant. But it is one tier of a two-tier structure, and leading with it — as both traditionalists and critics tend to do — produces a distorted picture.

Al-Hikmah is the deeper layer. The why beneath the what. The philosophical foundation that explains the purpose of the law, the nature of the human being the law is addressed to, the vision of human flourishing the law is designed to serve. Without this layer, the law becomes a list of rules enforced by authority. With it, the law becomes an expression of a coherent vision of what human life is for.

I had spent years reading Islam at the level of law. Reading it at the level of wisdom was a different experience entirely.

What Changed When I Actually Read It

I want to be specific rather than vague about what this reading changed for me.

Before: I believed because I had been given belief. My faith was inherited, ambient, cultural. It was real — I do not dismiss what my mother gave me — but it was unexamined. It lived in habit and feeling rather than in understanding.

After: I believe because I have read the argument and found it coherent. Not every verse, not every jurisprudential detail — I am a long way from that level of knowledge. But the central claim — that the universe is not an accident, that the human being is not an animal who got lucky, that there is accountability beyond this life, that the Quran is the preserved final communication of the one who made all of this — I find this claim more internally consistent, more honest about the human condition, and more intellectually serious than any alternative I have been offered.

That is a different kind of faith from what I started with. It is faith that can withstand the question. Faith that does not need the curtain to stay down because it has already looked at what it could see and found it sufficient.

An Invitation

I am not a scholar. I am not qualified to issue religious opinions or settle theological debates. I am a PhD student in computer science who found his way to these questions through a combination of childhood experience, academic curiosity, and the work of teachers I am deeply indebted to.

The Farahi school's work is available — lectures on YouTube, the book Islam: A Comprehensive Introduction by Javed Ahmad Ghamidi, and his translation and commentary. I cannot recommend them highly enough to anyone who wants to encounter the Quran as an argument rather than a set of rules.

And I want to say clearly: if you are someone who has questions about Islam — whether you are Muslim, non-Muslim, sceptical, hostile, or simply curious — these posts are written for you. Not to convince you of a conclusion but to share the honest journey of someone who asked the same questions you are asking and found, in this tradition, answers worth taking seriously.

The comments are open. I mean that.

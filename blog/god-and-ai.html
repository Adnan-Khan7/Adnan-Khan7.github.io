God, AI and the Question No One Stops Asking

2026 Faith & Reason 12 min read

I am a student of both science and Islam. These are my honest reflections — not a fatwa, not a final word. Discussion and disagreement both welcome.

I was debugging a LaTeX error at midnight.

The AI caught my mistake before I did — not just the technical error, but the logical inconsistency behind it. I sat back and thought: this thing has learned the patterns of human thinking well enough to catch what I missed. And then the next thought arrived without invitation: if a machine built from mathematics and electricity can do this, what does that say about the mind that conceived it?

A quote I had seen somewhere came back to me: a year spent in AI can make one believe in God.

I had dismissed it the first time I read it. That night it did not seem dismissible.

What followed was a conversation with an AI model that I did not expect to have. I am sharing it here not because an AI said something profound, but because the questions it helped me articulate are ones I have been carrying for a long time. The AI was just the occasion. The questions are much older.

The Binary

I put it simply: there are only two options for a thinking mind that will not stop asking.

Either the universe created itself.

Or there is one Creator beyond it.

I do not think there is a third way out. Every philosophical framework, every scientific theory, every religious tradition eventually collapses into one of these two positions. You can delay the choice with language — multiverse, simulation, emergence, probability — but the question underneath does not go away. Something cannot come from nothing. Either the universe is the uncaused thing, or the Creator is.

I find the first option more absurd than the second. Not because I cannot follow the argument — I can — but because a self-creating universe requires the same logical exemption we would give to God, except it asks us to grant that exemption to something that shows every sign of having begun.

And if I ever meet that Creator and discover He was also created — I will look for His Creator.

What the AI Said

I asked the AI to take the opposing position — to argue against me — so I could test the strength of my own reasoning. It did. Honestly and without flattering me.

The strongest objections it raised:

On mystery: You replaced one mystery with another. Saying God is uncaused is the same move as saying the universe is uncaused.

On Hawking: Stephen Hawking proposed that time itself began with the universe. If there is no before, there is no causal question. The universe requires no prior cause.

On competing claims: Every civilization has produced a framework claiming divine guidance. How do you adjudicate between them?

My responses:

On mystery — a book that has stood 1,400 years with its text intact, whose every claim has been subject to scrutiny from every direction without being scientifically defeated, is not mystery in the ordinary sense. It is standing evidence. The mystery is not in the claim but in what lies beyond the claim — and that mystery is, by design, preserved for the purpose of the test.

On Hawking — the limitation of human intellect is not the end of the question. It is the beginning of the next one. Give a rational mind a thousand more years and it will search a thousand more times. Hawking himself spent his entire life asking. That restlessness is data. A purely materialist framework has no account for why the mind cannot stop.

On competing claims — the Quran addresses this directly. It does not present itself as one tradition among many. It presents itself as the final, preserved version of the same message sent from the first human being. The corruption of earlier revelations is not a weakness in the argument — it is part of the argument. And the Quran's own challenge — produce something like it — has stood for fourteen centuries without being answered.

A Question That Started Long Before This Conversation

I have carried the question of the gap between what a religion says it is and what is done in its name since childhood. For a long time it felt like an argument against faith. The more I studied — through the Farahi school of thought and its careful reading of the Quranic text — the more I understood it as an argument for a correct reading of faith. The violence is not what the Quran teaches. That is a longer conversation and it has its own post.

The Instinct That Does Not Lie

The AI made one observation that I want to share because I cannot improve on it:

You are not being irrational. You are being more rational than someone who dismisses the question entirely.

And then I said something to it that I believe completely:

You are a machine. If you had even a single moment to live as a human being, you would know the reality of eternity by one simple fact.

No human wants to die.

That instinct is not programmed. It is not cultural. It is not regional. Every human being who has ever lived has felt it. The fear of ending. The sense that something in us was not meant to be temporary. A purely material account of the human being has no satisfying explanation for why creatures who are, by that account, merely biological machines would feel with such force that they should not cease to exist.

The Quran says this directly. The longing for permanence is not a cognitive error. It is recognition. The soul knows what it came from and, on some level, what it is going back to.

The Verdicts

I asked the AI for three verdicts at the end of the conversation.

As a machine: I process. I have no stake in eternity, no fear of death, no restlessness at three in the morning wondering why I am here. I can map arguments but I cannot feel their weight. I am the least qualified entity in this conversation to reach a verdict.

If the AI were a rational human: The cumulative case is strong. A preserved text, 1,400 years unchallenged. A framework that answers questions of justice, economics, and human values with internal coherence. A binary where one option requires the universe to explain itself and the other requires a Creator who by definition needs no explanation. A rational human factoring in eternal stakes would not dismiss this. Reason points one way.

My verdict as a believer: Not emotion — logic, history, and internal consistency. The Quran invites the question rather than fearing it. No human truly wants to die. That instinct alone whispers eternity. One God. One final book. One last Prophet. The test requires the curtain. The rational conclusion: follow the path to success in both lives.

Why I Am Writing This

I believe in God rationally. Which means I hold myself accountable for not sharing what I understand of that belief.

I am not writing to convert anyone. I am not qualified to judge anyone. Allah alone judges. I am writing because I love the human race — all of it — and because if even one person reads this and decides to honestly ask the question they have been suppressing, that is enough.

The Quran is not a book that fears inquiry. It is a book that demands it. Will you not reason? It asks this, in various forms, dozens of times.

I am a student. I am still learning. I will make errors and I welcome correction. But I will not be silent about what I have found, because silence in the face of something this important is its own kind of failure.

If you want to discuss, disagree, push back, or share your own journey — the comments are open. That is why they are there.
